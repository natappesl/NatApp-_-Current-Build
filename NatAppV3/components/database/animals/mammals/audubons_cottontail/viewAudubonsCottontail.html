Audubon's Cottontail

Sylvilagus audubonii

Overview

Audubon’s cottontail are a widespread western North American species that can survive arid landscapes. The common name is derived from their bushy white tails, which resemble cotton balls. They can run as fast as 15 mph, swim, and climb.

Identification

The Audubon’s cottontails are small (1.5 kg in weight and 33-43 cm in length). Females tend to be slightly larger than males. They are identified by their fluffy white tails and mixed brown-grey bodies.

Habitat

This species can be found throughout the VPGR and can even be seen grazing on campus lawns. They live and nest in underground holes, lined with grasses and fur.

Behavior

Audubon’s cottontails forage during early mornings and evenings, and spend the rest of their days in hiding. This species breeds between December and late summer.

Diet and Predators

Audubon’s cottontails primarily eats grasses. Predators include foxes, coyotes, badgers, bobcats, and hawks.
