Overview

Audubon’s cottontail are a widespread western North American species that can survive well in arid landscapes. Their common name is derived from their bushy white tails, which resemble cotton balls. They can run as fast as 15 mph and can also swim and climb.

Identification

Audubon’s cottontails are small (1.5 kg in weight and 33-43 cm in length). Females tend to be slightly larger than males. They are identified by their fluffy, white tails and mixed grey-brown bodies.

Habitat

Behavior

Audubon’s cottontails forage during early morning and evening and spend the rest of their day hiding. They breeds between December and late summer.

Diet and Predators

Audubon’s cottontails primarily eats grasses. Their predators include:

  • Foxes
  • Coyotes
  • Badgers
  • Bobcats
  • Hawks
